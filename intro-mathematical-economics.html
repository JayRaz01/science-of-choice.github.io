Introduction to the Governing Dynamics

"I came to the position that mathematical analysis is not one of many ways of doing economic theory: It is the only way. Economic theory is mathematical analysis. Everything else is just pictures and talk." - R. E. Lucas, Jr. (2001)

Understanding the Governing Dynamics

The governing dynamics are the mathematical principles that underpin the choices and decisions made by entities ranging from individuals like you and me to animals and firms. These dynamics provide a logical framework for understanding how decisions are made in a variety of contexts.

A famous scene in the movie A Beautiful Mind features John Nash, the mathematician often regarded as the father of governing dynamics, observing a flock of pigeons. He is depicted analyzing their movements, attempting to uncover patterns and reasoning behind their behaviour. However ridiculous this is, it highlights the essence of the governing dynamics: finding logical explanations for seemingly complex, or simple, behaviours.

The primary goal of studying the governing dynamics is to develop a structured framework that explains decision-making processes. While this framework can be applied to virtually any field, its most prominent use has been in mathematical economics—an approach that uses mathematical reasoning and quantitative methods to understand and build economic theories.

Unlike traditional qualitative theories that rely solely on intuition, the governing dynamics employ mathematical and statistical techniques to explain the psychological motivations behind choices. Much like how physical sciences use equations to describe natural laws, the governing dynamics aim to uncover the mathematical relationships that govern human decision-making.

Our Mathematical Models

When we construct equations that satisfy a set of assumptions and principles to describe or predict certain phenomena, we create what is known as a mathematical model. Broadly speaking, mathematical models can be categorized into two types: descriptive and predictive.

Descriptive Models: These models describe mathematical relationships without relying heavily on data. They tend to focus on traditional mathematics and are often driven by intuition.

Predictive Models: These models use large datasets to establish relationships and often involve statistical methods, particularly inferential statistics.

For the purpose of this discussion, we’ll focus on descriptive models, which align more closely with intuition and offer greater creative freedom. Let’s explore the fundamental assumptions and principles that such equations should include:

Key Assumptions and Principles:

  • Agents: The individuals or entities making choices.
  • Control Variables: The set of choices available to the agents.
  • State Variables: The state of the context under which these choices are made.
  • Value/Utility: The outcome of the choices made by the agents.
  • Rationality: The assumption that agents will always choose the best possible outcome.

By understanding and applying these foundational elements, we can begin to build models that describe the dynamics behind decision-making processes.

An Example: Eating Chocolate the Right Way

To illustrate the governing dynamics in a simple scenario, let’s consider two agents: Bob and Mary. They are each holding a chocolate bar and are trying to decide how many chunks he should eat. We can define this problem using the assumptions and principles discussed earlier:

  • Control Variable The control variable in this scenario is the number of chunks either individual eats from their chocolate bar.
  • State Variable: The state variable here is the quantity of chocolate remaining in either's possession as they eat.
  • Utility: The utility represents the enjoyment or satisfaction either individual derives from consuming the chunks of the chocolate bar.
  • Rationality: We assume that Bob and Mary will choose the number of chunks that maximizes their individual overall enjoyment, or utility.
